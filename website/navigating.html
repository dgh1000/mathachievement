Navigating your way home

I have compared a math problem to finding your way out of the woods. Many times, you will start out not knowing the answer—not knowing the direction to go. You will be confused.

We have seen it's okay to take a first step in any direction. Mistakes will happen along the way.

But you won't feel lost all the time. It's important that you develop a navigational sense. That means you have a feel for where things are.

Many centuries ago, ships in the open sea first learned to navigate. The surface of the ocean is the same in every direction—how do you know where you are? They measured the precise angles between heavenly bodies such as stars, the moon, and the sun, and from that computed their location. They could go straight toward their destination.

Navigation means three important things: knowing where you are going, knowing where you are now and knowing which direction you need to go.

Imagine you want to go to the Empire State Building, at 350 5th Avenue in New York, but you are currently lost. Before you can start moving, you need to know where you are right now.

Now suppose someone tells you that you are at the corner of 7th Avenue and 34th Street. You know from memory that the Empire State Building is in the Eastern direction. That helps, but is it enough yet? Suppose that it was totally dark and you were all turned around.

You would need someone to tell you which way East is.

To navigate, you need to know three things: (1) where the Empire State Building is, (2) where you are now, and (3) which way East is.

In the same way, to solve a math problem you need (1) a sense of the problem itself, (2) a sense of the what the solution will look like, and (3) a sense of what steps to take.

It's helpful to develop your own sense of these things, rather than depending on what the teacher says.

If I start outsourcing all my navigation to a little talking box in my car, I'm sort of screwed. I'm going to lose my car in the parking lot every single time. — Ken Jennings, greatest Jeopardy winner of all time

In New York or at sea, your precise location is important. Exact distances and directions are important. But in the woods you don't need as much precision. If you know your town is approximately over a hill and down a river, that's good enough. You'll get there.

Your sense of math is more like the woods. You will be taking guesses and doing experiments as you solve a problem. Of course, sometimes you will be go straight to the answer—but usually you will need to keep learning, both in math and in life.

In the woods, you know some basic facts about the lay of the land.

Suppose you want to go downhill. You can easily see or sense which way the Earth slopes away from you.

Suppose you are asked to solve this equation for \(x\):

$$ 3x + 2 = -2x + 12 $$

Note that \(x\) is on both sides. In Algebra, a common strategy is to get the \(x\) on one side only. If we subtract \(-2x\) from each side, like this

$$ \begin{aligned} 3x + 2 & = -2x & + 12 \\ -(-2x) \phantom{{}+2 {} } &\phantom{=} -(-2x) & \\ 5x + 2 & = & 12 \end{aligned} $$

we see that \(x\) is on the left side only. In that way, we are closer to the solution.

Think of it like this. You just took a step downhill. You can feel yourself getting closer. In life, when you are stuck trying to find a building, or remember a friend's phone number, or make a recipe, you usually look for a way to get unstuck. When you find it, you just feel, you just sense that it works. Math is sometimes a little strange compared to the rest of life, but you can develop that feel in math too.
